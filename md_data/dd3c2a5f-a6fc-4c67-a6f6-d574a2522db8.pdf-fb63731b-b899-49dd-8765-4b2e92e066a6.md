# Abstract

Optical neural networks (ONNs) have shown great promise in overcoming the speed and effi ciency bottlenecks of artifi cial neural networks (ANNs). However, the absence of high-speed, energy-effi cient nonlinear activators signifi cantly impedes the advancement of ONNs and their extension to ultrafast application scenarios like autonomous vehicles and real-time intelligent signal processing. In this work, we designed and fabricated a novel silicon-based ultrafast all-optical nonlinear activator, leveraging the hybrid integration of silicon slot waveguides, plasmonic slot waveguides, and monolayer graphene. We utilized double-balanced detection and synchronous pump-probe measurement techniques to experimentally evaluate the static and dynamic characteristics of the activators, respectively. Exploiting the exceptional picosecond scale photogenerated carrier relaxation time of graphene, the response time of the activator is markedly reduced to ${ \sim } 9 3 . 6$ ps. This response time is approximately fi ve times faster than electronic neural networks, establishing our all-optical activator as the fastest known in silicon photonics to our knowledge. Moreover, the all-optical nonlinear activator holds a low threshold power of 5.49 mW and a corresponding power consumption per activation of 0.51 pJ. Furthermore, we confi rm its feasibility and capability for use in ONNs by simulation, achieving a high accuracy of $9 6 . 8 \%$ for MNIST handwritten digit recognition and a mean absolute error of less than 0.1 dB for optical signal-to-noise ratio monitoring of high-speed optical signals. This breakthrough in speed and energy effi ciency of all-optical nonlinear activators opens the door to signifi cant improvements in the performance and applicability of ONNs, ushering in a new era of advanced artifi cial intelligence technologies with enormous potential.

# Introduction

In recent years, artifi cial intelligence (AI) has gradually penetrated the daily lives of the public, with examples of AI everywhere 1,2 . As a representative type of AI technology, artifi cial neural networks (ANNs), designed to emulate the information processing model of the human brain, are being rapidly developed to achieve high computing speeds and low power consumption to fulfi ll increasingly complex computing needs 3 . Currently, traditional electrical processors based on the von Neumann architecture face limitations in speed and energy effi ciency 4 . Consequently, meeting the ever-growing computing demand has become challenging. As a potential solution to overcome this technical bottleneck in ANNs, optical neural networks (ONNs), combining the high bandwidth and parallelism of photonic devices with the adaptability and complexity attained by methods similar to those seen in the brain, have gained signifi cant attention 5 . ONNs offer enticing advantages such as massive parallelism, making them a promising avenue to address the existing limitations of traditional electrical processors 6,7 .

The fundamental unit of an ONN is the optical neuron, which typically comprises two components: a linear operation component and a nonlinear operation component. Various devices, such as Mach-Zehnder interferometer (MZI) meshes 8 , are often utilized for the linear operation component to achieve high-speed and high-accuracy linear matrix vector multiplication operations in the optical domain. However, the diverse application scenarios achievable with ANNs cannot be fulfi lled by relying solely on

the linear operation component. Incorporating a component that can nonlinearly map the inputs of neurons to their corresponding outputs, i.e., activation functions, is essential. Consequently, achieving a nonlinear operation component with high speed, low power consumption, and high integration has long been pursued.

The nonlinear operation components developed thus far can be classifi ed into two main categories based on their operating mechanism: optoelectronic conversion 9,10 and all-optical components 11,12 . However, optoelectronic conversion methods face challenges in terms of processing speed, complexities and energy effi ciency, as they necessitate signal conversion among devices such as photodetector (PD), transimpedance amplifi er (TIA) and modulator, which demand supplementary electrical energy for sustained operation 13 . As alternatives to these methods, all-optical methods leverage the inherent advantages of optics, such as high processing speed and low power consumption, making them crucial for the advancement of neural networks. Consequently, to facilitate large-scale expansion and high-speed applications of neural networks, the adoption of all-optical methods is imperative.

Despite the challenges involved in realizing all-optical methods, particularly the relatively weak nonlinear effect within optical media, researchers have made signifi cant progress in recent years. Various approaches have been introduced to implement an all-optical nonlinear operation component. One notable development is the utilization of semiconductor optical amplifi ers (SOAs) for implementing the optical activation function (OAF). SOAs offer high gain saturation and short carrier lifetimes, and by harnessing these properties, researchers have been able to overcome the limitations of the weak nonlinear effects in optical media 14–17 . However, these works were all based on InP-based platforms, which are expensive and consume extensive power. Thus, concurrently, researchers have undertaken extensive endeavors on the silicon-based platform to resolve the challenges concerning OAF, as the platform is compatible with complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) technology and low-cost18,19 . For example, the silicon microring resonator (MRR) has been combined with various materials, such as phase change materials (PCMs)20,21 , and the nonlinear effects of these materials have been utilized to achieve OAFs. Although the use of an MRR enhances the nonlinearity of the device, there are still the shortcomings of slow response speed and narrow optical bandwidth, which limit processing capability of ONNs 22 . Indeed, the existing approaches for constructing OAFs all exhibit inherent defi ciencies that signifi cantly hinder the advancement of ONNs.

To address these challenges, we propose a novel solution for the implementation of activators based on the saturable absorption properties of graphene. Graphene, a two-dimensional material comprising carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice, possesses remarkable optoelectronic properties such as ultrahigh carrier mobility, a broad light absorption wavelength range, and a high thermal conductivity. These properties have enabled the development of various graphene-based optoelectronic devices with exceptional performance 23 . In particular, the saturable absorption resulting from Pauli blocking, combined with the ultrafast picosecond scale photogenerated carrier relaxation time within monolayer graphene, can facilitate the realization of ultrafast nonlinear activators. To fully exploit the advantages of the

ultrafast photogenerated carrier relaxation time of graphene, while maintaining a low threshold power, it is imperative to integrate graphene with structures that can enhance its absorption properties without causing excessive loss.

In this article, we combined graphene with double-slot waveguides to design an ultrafast all-optical nonlinear activator. The proposed activator leverages the saturable absorption and ultrafast carrier relaxation time of monolayer graphene, combined with the strong light confi nement of a double-slot structure. Double-balanced detection and synchronous pump-probe measurement techniques were employed to assess the static and dynamic characteristics of the fabricated activators, proving their low power consumption and ultrafast response time. Then, the applicability of the activator was verifi ed through two machine learning tasks. The experimentally measured OAFs are used for the MNIST handwritten digit recognition and optical signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR) monitoring tasks, achieving performance comparable to commonly used activation functions in ANN, such as ReLU. The results highlight the signifi cance of our all-optical silicon/graphene-based activator in performing critical calculations for ultrahigh-speed optical signals, representing a signifi cant advance in the fi eld of ONNs and paving the way for ONNs to be used in high-speed, low-power-consumption scenarios.

# Results

# Design of a silicon-graphene all-optical activator

Typically, ONNs are composed of at least one hidden layer, which encompasses a linear operation component and a nonlinear operation component. In common ONN schemes 24 , the linear operation is executed by means of an optical interference unit (OIU) comprising a programmable mesh of MZIs for weighting and summation (Fig. 1a). Each MZI is equipped with an internal phase shifter and an external phase shifter, as indicated by the red and blue markings, respectively. Following the OIU, an optical nonlinear unit (ONU), driven by our activators (Fig. 1b), is employed to perform the nonlinear operation. The computational process of ONNs can be succinctly expressed in a clear and concise mathematical form. Subsequently, $a _ { i } ^ { ( l ) }$ are conveyed to the ONU to accomplish nonlinear mapping $( x _ { j } ^ { ( l ) } = f ( a _ { i } ^ { ( l ) } ) )$ = , and the resultant signals are subsequently propagated to the next layer (Fig. 1c).

The proposed all-optical nonlinear activator consists of a silicon slot waveguide, a metallic slot waveguide, and monolayer graphene sandwiched vertically between the two waveguides (Fig. 1b). To attain an activator with low threshold power, it is essential to enhance the effective absorbance of graphene and mitigate the metallic ohmic absorbance of PSW with an appropriate double-slot structure. We utilized the fi nite element method (FEM) to optimize the structural parameters of the activator, as

detailed in the Supplementary Information (Section 1), and ultimately selected a gap width (W) of 90 nm and an Au height (H) of 25 nm based on the results. The all-optical nonlinear activator was then fabricated on a commercial SOI wafer to demonstrate the scheme, and the detailed fabrication process is described in the Materials and Methods. The optical microscopy image of the activators is depicted in Fig. 1d, while a false-color scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of the magnifi ed region is presented in Fig. 1e. To implement the OAF, the fundamental mode of the input strip waveguide is converted into the fundamental mode of a silicon slot waveguide via a mode converter25 . Subsequently, the light from the aforementioned waveguide is coupled into the double-slot waveguide. Following the nonlinear mapping process, the optical signals enter the strip waveguide in a reverse sequential manner. The length of the mode converter is $5 \mu \mathsf { m } ,$ the metallic part of the PSW consists of Au with a width of 100 nm, and the slot width is $9 0 \mathsf { n m }$ .

The details of the experimental setup are provided in the Materials and Methods. # Principle verifi cation of ultrafast silicon/graphene optical activator

We utilized the measured static response of the proposed nonlinear activator to implement a fully connected ONN for the classifi cation of MNIST handwritten digits using Keras, as this task is one of the benchmarking problems used to evaluate the performance of a neural network. The OAF used in this task was a rational interpolation function obtained from the experimental data, representing the relationship between power in and power out, as illustrated in Fig. S2 (Supplementary Section 2). The MNIST dataset is a collection of 70000 grayscale handwritten digit pictures from 0 to 9 with $2 8 \times 2 8$ pixels, of which 60000 pictures were used for training and the remaining 10000 were used for testing. Each image was fl attened into a one-dimensional array of 784 units that had been normalized between 0 and 1 and fed to the input layer of the neural network, as shown in Fig. 3a. Behind the input layer, there was a hidden layer consisting of 100 neurons, corresponding to $7 8 4 \times 1 0 0$ connections. The last layer had only ten neurons, representing ten types of output from 0 to 9. In our study, we utilized our proposed OAF to activate the input and hidden networks in a fully connected ONN for the MNIST handwritten digit classifi cation task. The SoftMax function was employed to activate the output layer and convert input values to probability distributions 32 . To replicate the conditions of ONNs, we imposed nonnegative constraints on the bias (b) and weight (w) during the training process 22 . To train the ONN, we implemented the backpropagation algorithm with the RMSProp optimizer and a batch size of 750 over the course of 500 epochs. The loss function employed was the cross-entropy loss function. We also compared the performance of our three OAFs with that of seven commonly used activation functions in electronic neural networks, including rectifi ed linear unit (ReLU) functions. Notably, the proposed OAFs outperform most of the commonly used activation functions used for comparison in terms of accuracy. The our2 activation function exhibits the best accuracy, with a training accuracy of $9 9 . 3 0 \%$ and a test accuracy of $9 6 . 8 0 \%$ (Fig. 3b, c and Table 1). And the confusion matrixes, illustrating the specifi c recognition probability of each number, of our2 for the training and test datasets are presented in Fig. 3d, e (confusion matrixes of others included in Supplementary Information Section 3). The highest prediction accuracy can reach up to $9 9 . 9 \%$ . This result demonstrates the feasibility of employing the proposed activators in an ONN.

# Dynamic characteristic of the silicon-graphene all-optical OAF

To evaluate the response bandwidth of our proposed activators, we utilized an asynchronous pump-probe measurement technique employing the dual-comb system (Fig. 7) to measure the dynamic characteristics 33,34 . The system employs a pair of mode-lock lasers with a small and fi xed difference in repetition rates as pump and probe sources. The pump pulse induces changes in the state of the activators, while the probe pulse monitors the transient behavior of the carriers. This method offers superior accuracy, detail and speed compared to conventional systems that use adjustable delay devices. The temporal transmission of the probe light as a function of the pump-probe delay time exhibited a fullwidth at half-maximum (FWHM) of approximately 93.6 ps, corresponding to a response bandwidth of ~ 10 GHz (Fig. 4), which is approximately fi ve times faster than electronic neural networks 35 . To our knowledge, this makes our activator the fastest known all-optical activator based on a silicon-based platform. To estimate the energy consumption, we consider an operation within an ONN as a spike event36 . A spike event means a neuron receives the stimuli spikes from the previous layer and then generates a response spike. We estimate the energy consumed by an operation by multiplying the threshold power by FWHM. Hence, for our1, the energy per activation amounts to approximately 0.51 pJ.

# OPM performance using an ONN with the ultrafast silicon/graphene optical activator

Based on the high response bandwidth (10 GHz) measured by our dynamic experiment, we explore the potential of our activator for high-speed application scenarios, such as optical performance monitoring (OPM) for optical communication networks. Optical communication networks currently play a pivotal role in modern communication systems, offering high-bandwidth, low-latency, and secure data transmission. To ensure the smooth and effective functioning of optical communication networks, the real-time quality of the optical signals transmitted across the networks must be continuously monitored and collected, that is, OPM must be performed. Given the exceptional performance of our ultrafast nonlinear activators, we proposed an ONN scheme (Fig. 5a) for high-speed OSNR monitoring, which is a crucial function in optical performance monitoring because of the straightforward relationship between the OSNR and the bit error

ratio (BER) of the transmission system32 . The proposed scheme includes a data arrangement unit that converts serial optical signals to parallel format and an ONN for high-speed OSNR monitoring. When the high-speed optical signal is transmitted to the receiver, a portion of the signal light is directed from the mixer of the coherent receiver to the data processing unit. Once the signal reaches a certain volume, it is converted to parallel format and fed into the ONN. The proposed ONN is composed of three layers: the fi rst and second layers contain 240 and 100 neurons, respectively, and employ the OAFs we designed, while the output layer consists of a linear neuron. This means that 120 signals are needed for each OSNR monitoring task. The weights and biases of the ONN are updated through the gradient descent method, optimized by Adam and the minibatch algorithm37 . It is important to note that despite the baud rate of the signal being 56 GBaud, the serial-to-parallel conversion is performed on 120 symbols of the 56 GBaud signals before entering the ONN. As a result, the bandwidth requirement for the ONN to complete a single OSNR monitoring task for the 56 GBaud signal is approximately 466 MHz (56 GHz/120). This requirement is signifi cantly lower than the proposed OAF's bandwidth of 10 GHz, indicating that the OAF exceeds the necessary bandwidth for the task.

We investigated the proposed OSNR monitoring scheme through numerical modeling using VPItransmissionMaker for 56 GBaud quadrature phase shift keying (QPSK)/16 quadrature amplitude modulation (16QAM)/64QAM optical signals transmitted over an 800 km SSMF. The transmitter comprised an external cavity laser (ECL) emitting a carrier with a central wavelength of 1550 nm and an I/Q modulator generating the optical signals, which required an optical power of 0 dBm. The optical signals were then transmitted through SSMF loops, and the recirculating loop consisted of an $8 0 ~ \mathsf { k m }$ SSMF with an attenuation coeffi cient of 0.2 dB/km, a nonlinear coeffi cient of $1 . 3 \mathrm { W ^ { - 1 } k m ^ { - 1 } }$ , a n d a chromatic dispersion coeffi cient of $1 6 . 8 \mathrm { p s } / ( \mathrm { n m } { \cdot } \mathrm { k m } ) ^ { 3 7 }$ . The OSNR of the optical signals was adjusted using a VOA and an erbium-doped fi ber amplifi er (EDFA) over a range of 6–12 dB with a step of 1 dB. Subsequently, the optical signals were coherently detected using a local oscillator laser at the receiver side, and the I and Q high-speed signals were digitally sampled after optoelectronic conversion. For the implementation of OSNR monitoring based on the ONN, $70 \%$ of the dataset was utilized for training, while the remaining $30 \%$ was reserved for testing.

Initially, we compare the response bandwidth and energy per activation (threshold power), as these factors determine the processing speed and energy effi ciency of ONNs. High processing speed and low power consumption constitute pivotal attributes for ONNs utilized in emerging applications that are unattainable by conventional computing technologies—those requiring high speed and low energies. Thus, these two parameters assume primary signifi cance for comparison. Generally, optoelectronic activators and active all-optical activators, comprising both silicon-based and InP-based ones, can achieve large response bandwidths. Nonetheless, these devices often necessitate supplementary energy

to sustain optimal operation, resulting in notable power consumption (as depicted by the fi rst three entries in Table 2). Conversely, passive all-optical activators relying on slow nonlinear phenomena like thermal effects exhibit the slowest response but consume less energy. Thanks to the superior carrier relaxation time of graphene and the optimal balance between the metal and graphene absorption of the double-slot structure, the passive silicon-based all-optical activator proposed in this work emerges as the most promising scheme for simultaneously fulfi lling the demands of high bandwidth and low power consumption.

The subsequent parameter to consider is the optical bandwidth. Unlike silicon-based nonlinear activators relying on resonance, which typically suffer from restricted optical bandwidth due to structural limitations, the activators proposed in this study showcase a broadband optical response (1547 nm-1560 nm). Furthermore, our devices possess the capability for seamless integration with linear networks without the requirement for supplementary components. This inherent fl exibility renders our activators suitable for diverse high-speed applications, such as optical communication networks.

Finally, we conduct a comparative analysis of these integrated devices based on their intuitionistic scale of physical size, as this parameter directly determines the degree of integration achievable in computing devices. Devices employing the optoelectronic conversion method typically manifest the largest footprint owing to the presence of electrical components, such as PD and TIA. Conversely, all-optical nonlinear devices, unencumbered by such constraints, often present the potential for further size reduction. Among these, passive silicon-based all-optical activators emerge as the most compact solution because of the high refractive index of silicon and the absence of electrical components, including our activators.

In conclusion, we proposed and fabricated a novel all-optical silicon/graphene-based nonlinear activator. The activator uses a double-slot structure that effectively enhances the interaction between light and graphene while minimizing optical losses. We exploited the ultrafast carrier relaxation time of graphene, resulting in an ultrafast response time of 93.6 ps, a response bandwidth of 10 GHz, a minimum threshold power of $5 . 4 9 \mathrm { m W } ,$ and a corresponding power consumption per activation of approximately 0.51 pJ. To our knowledge, this response time is the fastest among all previously reported all-optical integrated silicon nonlinear activators. Additionally, the device demonstrates an impressive optical bandwidth of 18 nm with a small footprint $( \sim 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } )$ , which enhances the fl exibility and integration capabilities of our device. Then, we demonstrated a benchmark machine learning task to validate the applicability of the activator. The experimentally measured OAFs were used for the MNIST handwritten digit recognition task, and a high accuracy of $9 6 . 8 \%$ was achieved. Furthermore, for the 56 GBaud QPSK/16QAM/64QAM optical signals transmitted by an 800 km SSMF, the MAE in OSNR monitoring was less than 0.1 dB. In the best case, the MAE was only 0.054 dB. Our research exhibits the potential of the developed activators to enable ultrafast applications for ONNs, such as autonomous vehicles and real-time intelligent signal processing, opening the door to signifi cant improvements in the performance and applicability of ONNs.

# Materials and Methods

# Device fabrication

The silicon-graphene nonlinear activator was fabricated on a commercial silicon-on-insulator (SOI) chip with a top silicon layer of 260 nm and a buried oxide layer of $1 \mu \mathrm { m }$ . Passive silicon waveguides were patterned using electron beam lithography (E-Beam Writer JBX-9500FSZ) and etched by a dry-etching process (SPTS-Pegasus). After the surface was planarized using chemical mechanical polishing (Logitech CM62 Orbis), the remaining silicon dioxide was thinned to zero thickness by advanced silicon etching (STS ICP Advanced Silicon Etcher). An $\mathsf { A l } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 3 }$ layer with a thickness of 7 nm was deposited on the sample using atomic layer deposition (Picosun R200), followed by the transfer of monolayer graphene (Graphene Supermarket) onto the sample using a photoresist (AZ 5214E, 2.2 µm). Graphene patterning was achieved by ultraviolet lithography (Maskless Aligner MLA150) and oxygen plasma etching. The PSW was defi ned using electron beam lithography, followed by deposition (Wordentec QCL800 Metal Evaporator) of Ti and Au layers and a lift-off process. Finally, the Au contact was defi ned using standard UV lithography, metal deposition and lift-off.

# Experimental setup for static characteristics measurement

The double-balanced detection experiment setup 26 employed a tunable laser source (TLS, CoBrite, DX4) to produce a narrow-linewidth light with a central wavelength of 1550nm (Fig. 6). The light is subsequently subjected to amplifi cation by an erbium-ytterbium-doped fi ber amplifi er (EYDFA, ShConnet, MFAP-ET-B-MP), followed by its passage through an optical bandpass fi lter (OBPF). Subsequently, the light passes through a variable optical attenuator (VOA, Joinwit, JW8506), the fi rst polarization controller (PC), and a polarization beam splitter in sequential order. Finally, the light is divided into two paths via a 95:5 optical coupler (OC), where $5 \%$ directly enters the optical power meter (OPM, Joinwit, JW3209C) as a reference power, while the remaining $9 5 \%$ passes through our nonlinear activators before reaching another optical power meter of the same model. Utilizing a computer, we swiftly and precisely scan the attenuation value of the VOA, while concurrently capturing the corresponding power values through two OPMs. It should be noted that each collection of power data is carried out after the VOA is adjusted and the OPMs are stabilized. Therefore, the setup can be used to measure the static characterization of our activators quickly and accurately. Furthermore, we employed the widely recognized cut-back method 41 , which relies on optical transmission measurements of a series of waveguides with varying lengths, to determine the total coupling loss. The total coupling loss, encompassing the coupling losses between the fi ber and the strip waveguide, between the strip waveguide and the silicon slot waveguide, and between the silicon slot waveguide and the double-slot waveguide, is 11.28 dB. This value serves as a basis for evaluating the nonlinear expression of the activator.

# Experimental setup for dynamic characteristics measurement

The detailed dual-comb system based on the asynchronous pump-probe measurement technique is illustrated in Fig. 7. The system consisted of a probe light with a wavelength range of 1546 nm to 1554

nm and a pump light with a range of 1558 nm to 1562 nm. Moreover, the repetition rates of the probe light and pump light were $f _ { p r o b e } = 1 0 0 M H \mathrm { z }$ and $f _ { p u m p } = f _ { p r o b e } + \Delta f = 1 0 0 . 0 0 1 M H z ,$ respectively, and the difference between them was 1 kHz, resulting in a time scaling factor of $M = f _ { p r o b e } / \Delta f = 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ . To narrow the pulse widths, we compensated for dispersion using a dispersion compensation fi ber (DCF) and a standard single-mode fi ber (SSMF), achieving pulse widths of 749 fs and 1.237 ps (see Supplementary Information Section 4 for details). The pump and probe lights were combined using an optical coupler and then fed into our activators, whose intensity was controlled by a variable optical attenuator (VOA). Two polarization controllers (PCs) were employed to optimize the coupling effi ciency by adjusting the polarization state. The transmitted probe light after passing through our devices was fi ltered by an optical bandpass fi lter (OBPF 3) and then captured by a 200-MHz photodetector (Discoveryoptica, PDD200MC30KFC) and a 500-MHz oscilloscope (RIGOL, DS4052) with a sampling rate of 4 GSa/s.
