Abstract—We propose a vibration event recognition method using fractional fourier transform (FrFT) and denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPM). The experimental results show that the model effectively identifies real vibration events by training on synthesized dataset, and it exhibits a high level of generalization ability.

Keywords—distributed vibration sensing, denoising diffusion probabilistic model, convolutional neural network, event recognition

# I. INTRODUCTION

The distributed fiber vibration sensing (DVS) system can continuously monitor vibrations distributed along the optical fiber. Additionally, it utilizes machine learning algorithms to identify and classify different vibration signals, achieving automated early warning capabilities. As a result, DVS finds wide application in various fields, including perimeter security [1], natural disaster prediction [2], and oil/gas pipeline monitoring [3]. In recent years, Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have received significant attention for their powerful capabilities in event recognition within the domain of DVS. For example, Stork et al. utilized a CNN algorithm based on YOLO3 to detect micro-seismic events [4]. In the dataset from which the real noise is taken the network is able to detect ${ > } 8 0 \%$ of the events identified by manual inspection. However, the majority of current research is based on discriminative CNN models. These approaches heavily rely on extensively labeled training data. Nevertheless, DVS applications often face challenges in signal acquisition, making it difficult to obtain sufficient training samples. The scarcity of training data leads to a certain degree of overfitting in CNN models.

In addition to discriminative CNN models, there are also generative CNN models, which have the ability to continuously generate synthesized data. In 2018, Shikoh et al. proposed a generative vibration event recognition method based on generative adversarial network (GAN) [5], which involved generating a large amount of synthesized data using GAN to augment the training dataset. Subsequently, they trained a discriminative model on this augmented dataset, achieving an average recognition accuracy of $8 3 \%$ and $8 0 . 2 \%$ on two sets of test experimental data, respectively. However, the quality, stability, and diversity of synthesized data generated by GAN models are insufficient. In 2020, a generative model called Denoising

Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) was proposed [6]. The core idea of DDPM is to use the diffusion process for training the generative model. The diffusion process is a stochastic process that gradually introduces noise to the original data, resulting in a series of data perturbed by noise. The objective of the model is to learn the probability distribution of the original data from these perturbed data points. Compared to GAN models, DDPM training is more stable, and the generated samples are more realistic and diverse.

This paper proposes a vibration event recognition method based on DDPM. We utilize the DDPM to generate timefrequency vibration data based on Fractional Fourier Transform (FrFT), making the generated synthesized data appear similar to real data. Subsequently, we train these synthesized data to detect real vibration events. Through experiments, this method demonstrates a high accuracy and generalization ability for real data.

# II. PROPOSED METHOD

The proposed approach consists of three steps: (1) Acquisition and processing of real vibration data, (2) Training and sampling of generative models, and (3) Design and training of discriminative models.

# A. Acquisition and processing of real vibration data

The DVS system used in this study is based on the direct detection $\Phi$ -OTDR (Phase-Sensitive Optical Time Domain Reflectometry) system, as shown in Fig. 1. Intrusion-induced vibrations cause changes in the optical path length and refractive index at the corresponding position of the optical fiber, resulting in a phase shift in the backscattered light, which is then detected by the DVS system. The system's spatial resolution is $5 \mathrm { m }$ , with a pulse repetition frequency of $1 \mathrm { k H z }$ . The experimental fiber length is $3 0 \mathrm { k m }$ .

We utilized this system to collect vibration signals caused by four different events: hammer, air pick, excavator operation, and ambient noise, in an outdoor setting. A lot of research has used time-domain or time-frequency domain features of vibration signals to form CNN training datasets. Time-domain signals only reflect the characteristics of vibration events from a single dimension, which can lead to limitations in single-dimensional recognition. Traditional time-frequency methods suffer from the problem of trade-off between time and frequency resolution. Therefore, we

introduce the Fractional Fourier Transform (FrFT) [7]. FrFT allows signals to be transformed into the fractional domain between the time and frequency domains. By continuously scanning through the fractional domain between time and frequency, it increases the dimensions of signal analysis, which is beneficial for the learning process of CNN models.

# B. Training and sampling of generative models

DDPM can be generalized into two main processes: forward diffusion and parameterized reverse diffusion. Fig. 3 illustrates the two directions of the diffusion probability model. In the forward process, the model utilizes Gaussian noise kernels to progressively generate noisier samples from the time-frequency data. In the reverse process, the model iteratively retrieves data from noise, which can be used to generate synthesized data from random noise.

We built the model and set up the parameters in an environment with Python 3.8 and PyTorch 2.0. The learning rate is set to 0.0001, the batch size is 8 limited by GPU. We use 3 residual layers in a diffusion decoder, where the number of convolution channels are 128. The total number of diffusion steps is 4000, the noise adding mode is cosine.

![](images/f5f1ea8d9968953065227ef415ab2c7c1290875373e57270dfddf99c1c6ee42a.jpg)  
Fig. 3. The reverse process and the diffusion/forward process in a diffusion probabilistic model. The diffusion/forward process (dotted arrow) gradually converts data into noise within finite steps. The reverse process (solid arrow) is a parametric procedure which attempts to restore data structure from noise.

# C. Training of discriminative models

The synthesized data generated by DDPM was compiled into a dataset, comprising a total of 15,000 samples. The dataset was randomly divided into training, validation, and test sets following an 8:1:1 ratio.

The discriminative model used in this study is the ResNet18 model [9]. ResNet addresses the issues of gradient vanishing and network degradation during the training process of deep neural networks, thereby enabling the learning of richer and more abstract feature representations. In classification tasks, this ability is crucial for distinguishing different event categories. The learning rate is set to 0.001, the batch size is 64, the backpropagation algorithm is Adam, and the loss function is the crossentropy loss function. To prevent overfitting due to a long training period, we choose the epoch with the lowest loss function on the validation set as the optimal epoch, and the model at that epoch is considered the best model.

Table 1 summarizes the classification results of the testset, achieving an accuracy of $9 2 . 7 9 \%$ . These results are based on synthesis data. To evaluate the performance of the proposed method on real data, we prepared two new testsets. The first testset (testset 1) comprised the original real data used to train DDPM initially, containing 13,649 samples. The second testset (testset 2) consisted of new vibration signals collected after a one-week interval, with a total of 1,175 samples. This is because DVS signals exhibit nonstationarity and time-varying characteristics, and if the model's generalization ability is not strong, the recognition accuracy of the system may significantly decrease over time. The testset 2 was used to verify the model's generalization performance.

# IV. CONCLUSION

In this paper, we propose a novel vibration event recognition method using DDPM, This method is capable of generating a large number of synthesized data as the training dataset, resulting in a high recognition rate for real vibration events and strong generalization ability.
