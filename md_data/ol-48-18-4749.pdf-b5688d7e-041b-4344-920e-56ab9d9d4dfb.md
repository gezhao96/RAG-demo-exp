# Endogenous BOTDA in the self-homodyne coherent detection transmission system for communication-integrated datacenter temperature monitoring

Received 5 June 2023; revised 16 August 2023; accepted 18 August 2023; posted 22 August 2023; published 8 September 2023

This Letter demonstrates the high compatibility of the selfhomodyne coherent detection (SHCD) transmission system with the Brillouin optical time-domain analyzer (BOTDA). By fully utilizing the remote delivered local oscillator (LO) light of the transmission system, the first, to the best of our knowledge, endogenously integrated BOTDA subsystem is achieved. The remote delivery of the homologous laser source in the SHCD system ensures the frequency match between the probe light and the pump light of the BOTDA. Furthermore, an injection-locked distributed feedback (DFB) laser is employed to amplify the LO and eliminate the impact induced by the Brillouin gain. The experiment demonstrates that a 16-km distributed temperature sensing based on BOTDA can be insensibly emerged into a 50-Gbaud DP-16QAM SHCD transmission system (400 Gbps/λ/core), achieving a spatial resolution of 3 meters and a temperature accuracy of $\mathbf { 1 } ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } .$ Remarkably, the auxiliary sensing module has negligible impact on the transmission. $\circledcirc$ 2023 Optica Publishing Group

In today’s digital era, data centers (DCs) have become a crucial component of our information infrastructure. As Internet traffic continues to surge and the demand for high-performance computing grows, DCs are becoming more complex and generating higher levels of heat dissipation. Effective temperature management is therefore essential to ensure the efficient operation of servers and other equipment. Furthermore, cooling systems can consume up to $3 7 \%$ or more of a DC’s total electricity usage, making them a significant contributor to energy consumption and associated costs [1]. Thus, it is essential to identify innovative solutions that can balance energy consumption and cooling effectiveness for the sustainable future of DCs.

Temperature monitoring is a critical challenge in the efficient cooling system of DCs. Conventional wired temperature probes and wireless sensor networks (WSNs) are commonly used for thermal monitoring [2,3]. However, wired solutions can

only provide temperature data at a coarse level, while wireless solutions may be affected by electromagnetic interference in DC rooms. Moreover, as the size and complexity of DCs continue to increase, the cost of numerous probes can be significant due to an enlarged monitoring area. Optic-fiber-based sensors, such as fiber Bragg gratings (FBGs), exhibit advantages such as immunity to electromagnetic interference, high accuracy, and robust broad-range temperature monitoring, making them an attractive option for quasi-distributed temperature monitoring [4]. Despite this, FBG sensors face challenges when applied to large areas such as DC facilities. Distributed optical fiber sensing (DOFS) is a highly promising method for gathering environmental information. One of the most conventional and mature techniques within this field is based on stimulated Brillouin scattering (SBS) [5]. Even a basic form of Brillouin optical time-domain analyzer (BOTDA) can provide distributed temperature sensing (DTS) with a range of approximately 30 kilometers, a resolution of several meters, and a temperature accuracy of approximately $1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ [6], thus making it a suitable option for temperature monitoring in DCs. However, the BOTDA comes with certain drawbacks, such as the need for access to both ends of the sensing fiber to inject the pump and probe, and the requirement for precise frequency matching, which makes it complex and costly.

Integration of sensing and communication (ISAC) has become a subject of great interest lately. By using existing devices and links for data transmission, cost-effective and easily deployable DOFS systems can be developed. While research on ISAC has been conducted, especially for vibration sensing using phasesensitive optical time-domain reflectometry $\Phi$ -OTDR) [7,8], an effective ISAC solution for DTS systems remains an open issue, despite its greater importance than vibration sensing in the DC scenario. Meanwhile, DC interconnection solutions are transitioning from 400G to higher rates, and the need to balance high-speed and low power consumption has resulted in the development of several new short-reach communication architectures [9,10]. The self-homodyne coherent detection (SHCD) solution is a low-cost option for achieving simplified coherent interconnection in DCs. Because the local oscillator (LO) is delivered

In this Letter, we report on the integration of a BOTDA sub-system into an SHCD transmission system for distributed temperature sensing in DCs. The BOTDA can be operated as a value-added and independent module, which shares the same laser source with the transmission system, but has negligible impact on the transmission performance.

In BOTDA systems, two light beams are launched into the sensing fiber from opposite ends. The co-propagating beam called the pump at frequency $\nu _ { 1 }$ interacts with the counterνpropagating beam called the probe at frequency $\nu _ { 0 }$ and arouses a co-propagating acoustic wave at frequency $\nu _ { 1 } - \nu _ { 0 }$ . The couν νpling between the two optical waves occurs due to diffraction from the refractive index perturbation produced by the acoustic field, while energy is supplied to the acoustic wave as a result of the interference pattern between the two optical waves through the process of electrostriction. As the pump propagates along the fiber, the probe obtains power from it whenever $\nu _ { 0 } = \nu _ { 1 } - \nu _ { B }$ , where $\nu _ { B }$ ν ν νis known as the Brillouin frequency, which is depenνdent on both the strain and temperature of the fiber [5]. The Brillouin frequency shift (BFS) varies linearly with the strain and the temperature, as given by [15]

$$
\Delta v _ {B} = C _ {\varepsilon} \Delta \varepsilon + C _ {\theta} \Delta \theta , \tag {1}
$$

where $\Delta { \nu } _ { B }$ is the BFS, $\Delta \varepsilon$ is the strain, $\Delta \theta$ is the temperature, and $C _ { \varepsilon }$ νand $C _ { \theta }$ ε θare their coefficients, respectively.

In the SHCD architecture, the data signal lights are propagating along with the LO laser light. When the received LO is relative-time-delay (RTD) matched with the signal light, it simplifies the need for DSP including frequency offset estimation (FOE) and carrier phase recovery (CPR) [16]. In the proposed scheme, the LO beam is a continuous wave (CW) power-stabled light, which also act as the probe in the BOTDA sub-system. The LO recovery unit can amplify the remote delivered LO with adequate power, despite any attenuation that may occur in the fiber link. The recovered LO is split into two tributaries for coherent receiving and pump pulse generating. As shown in Fig. 1(b), in the pump pulse generation module, one tributary of the recovered LO is passed through an electro-optic modulator (EOM) to induce a frequency shift, and then filtered by a fiber Bragg grating (FBG) filter. It is noteworthy that the frequency and power stability of the pump and the probe in this system are strictly ensured owing to the homogeneous recovered light source. Another function of the LO recovery unit is to eliminate the interference from the Brillouin gain. The LO recovery unit works as a narrowband filter, which reduces the influence of the pump backscattering light, as shown in Fig. 1(c). In detail, the LO recovery is leveraged by the optical injection locking (OIL) effect [17]. Herein, an injection-locked distributed feedback (IL-DFB) laser is adopted, which is a commercial DFB module without an optical isolator. The IL-DFB laser works as a “slave” laser while the laser source in the transmitter acts as its “master”. When the master laser carrier frequency (wavelength) is close enough to the free-running lasing carrier frequency (wavelength) of the slave laser, the slave laser will be forced to synchronize with the master laser, i.e., lasing at the same frequency with a fixed phase offset. The slave laser will also follow any slow frequency drift of the master laser with a relatively constant output power. Meantime, the slave laser will reduce the linewidth of injected laser, which leads to a narrowband filter of the unwanted interference.

A variable optical attenuator (VOA1) is used to control the launch power of the LO. An EDFA and a variable optical attenuator (VOA2) are used additionally for a wider sweeping range of the received optical power (ROP). The EDFA is optional, because the ROP will be up to $- 1 5 \ : \mathrm { d B m }$ , even if this EDFA is omit, and the whole system will still operate well. A 16-km multicore fiber link is used, where the inner core transmits the LO and enables the BOTDA, while one of the outer cores is used to transmit the data signal. The inner core is specially chosen to prevent the mechanism effect of random bending and twisting of the fiber coiling, which will cause the random variation of BFS [18]. The total insertion loss of the MCF link, including the fan-in and fanout loss, is approximately 6 dB. The loss differences between the cores are negligible. Leveraged by the low temperature-induced skew between cores [19], the RTD of the LO and the signal is static, enabling the free-of-phase-noise SHCD transmission. At the receiving end, a static optical delay line is used to match the invariant RTD between the signals and the LO. An IL-DFB is used to amplify the power of the remotely delivered LO stably from less than $- 1 0 ~ \mathrm { d B m }$ to 14 dBm. Meanwhile, the intensity fluctuation caused by the BOTDA is eliminated. The signal and the LO are detected using an integrated coherent receiver (Neo Photonics $\mu { 4 0 }$ ) and acquired by a skew-matched DSO at 80-GSaps sampling rate. The offline-DSP algorithms include orthonormalization, resampling, constant modulus algorithm (CMA), and static phase rotation, while avoiding frequency offset estimation and carrier phase recovery.

For the sensing part, the BOTDA system is designed to be an endogenously integrable module over the LO lane. Following the IL-DFB, an optical coupler (OC2) is used to split the regenerated zero-frequency-offset laser source. The split source light is modulated by a Mach–Zehnder modulator (MZM) to obtain two sidebands at Brillouin frequency $\nu _ { B }$ . The lower modulated νsideband is subsequently eliminated by a $0 . 1 \mathrm { - n m }$ bandwidth FBG optical filter. Then, the retained upper sideband light is used to generate the pump pulses via an acousto-optic modulator (AOM). The pulse width is set to 30 ns due to the rise time limitation of the AOM. To avoid the polarization impact, a polarization scrambler is used. The pump pulses are then boosted by an EDFA and injected to the LO lane reversely via CIR1. The SBS effect in the LO lane is facilitated by the counter-propagating remotely delivered LO CW light and the frequency-shifted pump pulses. The SBS-enhanced LO light will be split by OC1 and detected by a photodiode. The reflectometry waveform of the pump can be captured by an oscilloscope. For denoising, 512-time averaging is adopted.

In conclusion, we propose and demonstrate that the SHCD transmission system is highly compatible with the Brillouin optical time-domain analyzer. We achieved the first endogenously integrated BOTDA sub-system, which shares the same laser source, in a 16-km MCF SDM-SHCD transmission system. The distributed temperature sensing system is merged into a 50-Gbaud dual-polarization 16-QAM transmission system (400 Gbps/λ/core). The proposed BOTDA module provides a sensing range of $1 6 \mathrm { k m }$ with a spatial resolution of 3 meters and a temperature accuracy of $1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Furthermore, the value-added BOTDA is insensibly converged with the SHCD system without

performance degradation to the communication function. By passively taping the LO light, it does not need an additional laser source thus making it possible to agilely access different interconnection link and monitor the temperature accordingly.
