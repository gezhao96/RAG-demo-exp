# 1. Introduction

In recent years, distributed fiber optic vibration sensing (DVS) has found widespread application in various fields, including seismic monitoring [1], perimeter monitoring [2], and oil/gas pipeline monitoring [3]. Researchers have leveraged the real-time vibration monitoring capability of DVS to collect a significant amount of vibration data in different application scenarios requiring identification. Following preprocessing and manual feature extraction, deep learning algorithms such as convolutional neural networks (CNN) and long short-term memory networks (LSTM) are employed to analyses these data, enabling the system to automatically classify various interference vibration events.

Currently, model training for vibration data typically utilizes time-domain signals, frequency-domain signals, or time-frequency domain signals obtained from DVS. However, time-domain and frequency-domain signals only provide event characteristics from a single dimension, limiting their recognition capabilities. Time-frequency domain signals provide information about the vibration characteristics in both the time and frequency domains, allowing us to observe the frequency changes of signals over time. However, when dealing with non-stationary signals like DVS signals, traditional time-frequency transformation methods such as the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) suffer from inherent time-frequency uncertainty. This is because non-stationary signals exhibit varying characteristics over time. As a result, these traditional methods may not effectively capture the transient features of non-stationary signals. In addition, wavelet transform methods can convert signals from the time domain to the wavelet coefficient domain, capturing the characteristics of signals in different frequency ranges. However, the fixed nature of the basis functions limits their adaptability to signals.

To address these challenges, we propose a DVS signal recognition method based on the fractional Fourier Transformation (FrFT). This method exploits the characteristics of FrFT to explore the fractional domain between the time and frequency domains, thereby providing the deep learning model with richer time-frequency features. Field experiments conducted on the recognition of three typical events in urban cable safety monitoring demonstrate that the proposed method achieves high recognition accuracy and generalization capability simultaneously.

# 2.1. Dataset Preprocessing and FrFT

In the underground cable perimeter monitoring depicted in Fig. 1, three typical vibration events along the cable were chosen as the objects for identification: crowbar impact, air pick operation, and subway passing. The data preprocessing primarily involves removing the DC component and normalization. By subtracting the signal's mean value, the DC component can be eliminated, resulting in a time-domain signal with a mean close to zero. This approach allows the focus during time-frequency transformation to be directed towards the signal's dynamic changes and transient characteristics. Normalization scales the time-frequency data obtained after FrFT to a range of 0 to 1, thereby unifying dimensional differences between various features and mapping the data to a similar numerical range.

Due to its rotational property [4], the FrFT is capable of scanning the angular direction of the time-frequency distribution, making it suitable for analyzing non-stationary signals. The FrFT of a signal $f ( t )$ with a rotation angle $\alpha _ { \ast }$ , denoted as $F _ { \alpha } ( u )$ ,is defifined as

$$
F _ {\alpha} (u) = \int_ {- \infty} ^ {+ \infty} f (t) K _ {\alpha} (t, u) d t \tag {1}
$$

where the transform kernel $K _ { \alpha } ( t , u )$ of the FrFT is given by

$$
K _ {\alpha} (t, u) = \left\{ \begin{array}{c c} \sqrt {\frac {1 - j \cot \alpha}{2 \pi}} \exp \left(j \frac {t ^ {2} + u ^ {2}}{2} \cot \alpha - \frac {j t u}{\sin \alpha}\right) & \alpha \neq n \pi \\ \delta (t - u) & \alpha = 2 n \pi \\ \delta (t + u) & \alpha = (2 n \pm 1) \pi \end{array} \right. \tag {2}
$$

Compared to other time-frequency transformation methods, the FrFT allows signals to be transformed into the fractional domain between the time domain and the frequency domain. By continuously scanning the fractional domain between the time and frequency domains, it adds more dimensions to the signal analysis, which benefits the learning process of deep learning algorithms and effectively reduces the occurrence of overfitting. Additionally, the FrFT can adjust the angle to alter the signal's resolution in the frequency domain, enabling a trade-off between time and frequency resolutions as needed.

As mentioned earlier, FrFT possesses rotational characteristics, which can be used to scan the angular direction of the time-frequency distribution. The specific process involves forming a polar representation of the time-frequency distribution images using a set of received FrFT training sequences with different orders. These images are then transformed from polar coordinates to Cartesian coordinates using Radon inverse transform for further analysis. The FrFT is applied to the time-domain signals with the DC component removed, and time-frequency distribution images for different vibration events are constructed using FrFT TSs of length 256. The received FrFT training sequences are FrFT-transformed within the range of 0 to 1 order, with a scanning step of 0.1.

# 2.2. Network Construction and Model Training.

The deep learning model used in this study is the ResNet18 model [5], which is a type of deep residual network. ResNet addresses the issues of gradient vanishing and network degradation during the training process of deep neural networks, thereby enabling the learning of richer and more abstract feature representations. In classification tasks, this ability is crucial for distinguishing different event categories.

We built the model and set up the parameters in an environment with Python 3.8 and PyTorch 1.7. The learning rate is set to 0.0001, the batch size is 128, the backpropagation algorithm is Adam, and the loss function is the crossentropy loss function. The training is performed for 50 epochs. To prevent overfitting due to a long training period, we choose the epoch with the lowest loss function on the validation set as the optimal epoch, and the model at that epoch is considered the best model.

# 3. Experiments and results

# 3.1. Field Experiments and Datasets

The DVS device was connected to the communication optical cable in Guangzhou city, China. The cable has a total length of $1 2 . 5 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ and is buried underground at a depth of $0 . 5 { - } 1 . 5 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ . The spatial resolution is $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ , with a pulse repetition frequency of $1 \mathrm { k H z }$ . The time sampling interval is 1 ms, and the spatial sampling interval is $1 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m }$ . For data collection, three typical vibration events were selected. Fig. 2 illustrates the preprocessed time-domain

signal curves of these three events. It can be observed that the crowbar event is an instantaneous event with a short duration. The air pick event and subway event cause significant variations in the signal throughout the entire time window, with the vibrations from the air pick being slightly more regular compared to the subway.

The FrFT method was applied to extract preliminary features from the signals. Fig. 3 displays the time-frequency spectrogram obtained after performing the FrFT on the signals collected from different randomly selected events. It can be observed that the crowbar event exhibits significant changes in amplitude during the impact, with frequencies primarily distributed in the mid-to-low range. The air pick and subway events cause significant variations across the entire time-frequency spectrogram. Each time-frequency data has a size of $2 5 6 \mathrm { x } 2 5 6$ , and compared to the timefrequency data generated by STFT, it contains richer time-frequency features.

The aforementioned time-frequency data were compiled to form the FrFT time-frequency dataset, consisting of 4000 samples for each event. The dataset was randomly divided into training, validation, and testing sets in an 8:1:1 ratio. For comparison purposes, a time-frequency dataset based on STFT was also constructed using the same event signals. As mentioned earlier, DVS signals belong to non-stationary signals, and their statistical characteristics vary over time. Therefore, the randomly partitioned test set from the dataset may not adequately reflect the model's generalization ability. To address this, a new test dataset was collected, comprising 700 samples for each of the three types of vibration events, with a two-week interval. This new test dataset is referred to as the generalization test set.

This suggests that the rich time-frequency features provided by FrFT effectively assist the deep learning algorithm in distinguishing the non-stationary signals in DVS.

# 4. Conclusion

This paper proposes a novel method for DVS signal recognition based on FrFT. The results of field experiments demonstrate that, for deep learning algorithms, the time-frequency signals generated by FrFT, as proposed in this paper, outperform those generated by STFT in terms of recognition accuracy and generalization capability.
