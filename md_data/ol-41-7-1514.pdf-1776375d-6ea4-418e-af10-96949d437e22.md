# Few-mode fiber based distributed curvature sensor through quasi-single-mode Brillouin frequency shift

Received 9 February 2016; revised 20 February 2016; accepted 23 February 2016; posted 23 February 2016 (Doc. ID 259202); published 25 March 2016

We proposed and demonstrated a few-mode fiber (FMF) based optical-fiber sensor for distributed curvature measurement through quasi-single-mode Brillouin frequency shift (BFS). By central-alignment splicing FMF and singlemode fiber (SMF) with a fusion taper, a SMF-componentscompatible distributed curvature sensor based on FMF is realized using the conventional Brillouin optical time-domain analysis system. The distributed BFS change induced by bending in FMF has been theoretically and experimentally investigated. The precise BFS response to the curvature along the fiber link has been calibrated. A proof-of-concept experiment is implemented to validate its effectiveness in distributed curvature measurement. $\circledcirc$ 2016 Optical Society of America

Shape measurement and rebuilding of objects are now widely demanded in a variety of applications, such as reverse engineering, computer vision, online product inspection, and medical diagnosis [1]. For an accurate shape measurement, curvature is an essential parameter to sketch the flatness and curvature rate. Recently, various fiber-based curvature sensors with high sensitivity and different sensing ranges have been proposed, such as a long period fiber grating [2,3], fiber Bragg grating (FBG) [4], tilted FBG [5], Sagnac loop [6], directional coupler [7], and Mach–Zehnder interferometer [8]. However, all the aforementioned methods are based on single-point or discrete sensing technologies, which cannot perceive the continuous variation of curvature along the whole fiber link and lose some crucial information in the detection environment. In this Letter, dissimilar to the traditional curvature sensors and imitating the quasi-single-mode operation of few-mode fiber (FMF) in the

long-haul optical fiber communication demonstration [9], we propose a distributed curvature sensing system based on the online monitoring of Brillouin frequency shift (BFS) in FMF. Unlike in single-mode fibers (SMFs) [10,11], the BFS variation of the fundamental mode in the FMF is much more sensitive and has the ability to distinguish the curvature rate in a larger range, thus enabling the quantitative measurement of curvature. To implement the online distributed BFS measurements, we establish a Brillouin optical time-domain analysis (BOTDA) system with SMF-based components, which makes the system easy to construct and cost effective. By central-alignment splicing SMF and FMF with a fusion taper, quasi-single-mode operation in FMF has been achieved; thus, the SMF components can compatibly work with FMF with low splicing losses. And we experimentally proved that the higher order modes in the FMF are effectively suppressed. A BFS variation coefficient of $1 1 . 4 9 ~ \mathrm { M H z } \cdot \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ in the curvature radius range of $0 . 7 { - } 3 . 4 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ has been achieved.

In optical fiber, Brillouin scattering is inelastically scattered light with acoustic phonons and suffers a Doppler shift $\nu _ { B }$ , called the BFS, during particle collision. If the introduced frequency probe wave equals the downshift frequency of Stokes, stimulated Brillouin scattering (SBS) would happen. The BFS $\nu _ { B }$ is linear with strain and temperature [12], as given by

$$
\Delta \nu_ {B} = A \Delta T + B \Delta \epsilon , \tag {1}
$$

where $A$ and $B$ are the temperature and strain coefficients, respectively.

When bending happens in the optical fiber, a lateral offset of the optical beam will arise, which is given by [10,13]

$$
d = \frac {a ^ {2} V ^ {2}}{4 \mu R} (0. 6 5 + 1. 6 1 9 V ^ {- 1. 5} + 2. 8 7 9 V ^ {- 6}) ^ {4}, \tag {2}
$$

where a is the radius of the fiber core, $R$ is the radius of curvature, $V$ is the normalized frequency defined by $V = \mathrm { k a n } _ { 1 } \mu ^ { 1 / 2 } ,$ , $\mu =$ $[ 1 - ( n _ { 2 } / n _ { 1 } ) ^ { 2 } ]$ , $k$ is the free-space wave number, and $n = n _ { 1 , 2 }$

are the refractive indices in the core and the cladding, respectively. From solid mechanics, in the plane of curvature, the bending introduces a position-dependent strain across the fiber section; the strain $\epsilon$ induced by a curvature with radius $R$ is given by [10,14]

$$
\epsilon = \left(\frac {1}{R}\right) y, \tag {3}
$$

where $y$ is the distance from the fiber axis. Therefore, when the fiber is bending, we can estimate the bending strain at the beam position with the lateral displacement, which means the $d$ from Eq. (2) equals the y from Eq. (3), and combining Eq. (1), when the temperature is constant, the bending-induced BFS change can be expressed as

$$
\Delta \nu_ {B} = \frac {a ^ {2} V ^ {2} B}{4 \mu R ^ {2}} (0. 6 5 + 1. 6 1 9 V ^ {- 1. 5} + 2. 8 7 9 V ^ {- 6}) ^ {4} = \frac {M}{R ^ {2}}, \tag {4}
$$

where we define $M$ , which is equal to $a ^ { 2 } V ^ { 2 } B ( 0 . 6 5 +$ $1 . 1 6 1 9 V ^ { - 1 . 5 } + 2 . 8 7 9 V ^ { - 6 } ) ^ { 4 } / 4 \mu$ , as the BFS variation coefficient of the curvature radius depending on the curved fiber parameters. With a larger $M$ the BFS of a fiber would be more sensitive to bending.

For the conventional SMF, Minardo et al. have theoretically and experimentally confirmed that the dependence of BFS on curvature can be neglected [10]. On the other hand, in the multimode fiber (MMF), the BFSs are dissimilar for different spatial modes [15]; the mode conversion between the complicated modes and intermodal dispersion will cause tremendous sensing interference, especially when the bending happens [16]. Compared with MMF, FMF supports only a limited number of spatial modes (normally two to six modes), and the coupling between the LP01 mode and other higher order modes can be largely suppressed with careful design [17].

We designed and fabricated the weak mode-coupling FMF with a step-index profile. The fiber has a core radius of $9 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ and a cladding diameter of $1 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ . The refractive indices of core and cladding are 1.46111 and 1.45601, which means the normalized frequency is about 4.45 to support four linear polarization (LP) modes at the wavelength of $1 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . The attenuation coefficient of the FMF is $\mathrm { \bar { 0 } } . 2 \ \mathrm { d B / k m }$ for quasisingle-mode transmission. As the BFS sensitivity to strain of the fundamental mode in a similar FMF is reported around $0 . 0 5 8 ~ \mathrm { M H z / \mu \epsilon }$ [18], we can estimate its bending-induced BFS variation coefficient $M$ to be around $1 5 . 5 ~ \mathrm { M } \mathrm { \bar { H } z } \cdot \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ . The theoretical BFS change as a function of the curvature radius ranging from 0.8 to $2 . 8 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ is shown as the red curve in Fig. 1. As a contrast, the bending-induced BFS change of a conventional SMF with a core radius of $4 . 1 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and $\mu$ of 0.00697 is plotted as the blue curve in the same figure. Compared with the SMF, whose BFS variation coefficient of curvature radius is $3 . 6 6 ~ \mathrm { M H z } \cdot \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ , FMF has a much larger BFS sensitivity on radius variation.

In order to perform distributed curvature measurements along the FMF, a typical BOTDA system is constructed, and the experimental setup is illustrated in Fig. 2. We use a narrow linewidth $\scriptstyle < 1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ k H z } )$ external-cavity laser as an optical source with a wavelength located near $1 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . It is split into two paths by a 3-dB coupler for probe and pump. The probe wave in the upper path first passes through an electro-optic modulator (EOM1), which is biased at the null point and driven by a microwave synthesizer with a sine wave at frequency $\nu _ { F }$ to get the frequency-shifted optical signal. The output from EOM1 with a 30 dB carrier suppression ratio is polarization scrambled

It is then launched into one side of the fiber under test (FUT) with an average optical power of $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m W }$ . The pump wave in the lower path is first modulated to a pulsed signal of 28 dB extinction ratio by EOM2, driven by an arbitrary function generator (AFG, Tektronix AFG3102). The pulse duration is 10 ns, corresponding to a 1 m spatial resolution in a silica fiber. The optical pulse is then amplified by an erbium-doped fiber amplifier (EDFA) to boost the peak optical power to $2 . 3 \mathrm { W }$ and is then fed into the other side of the FUT through a circulator (CIR). After the third port of the CIR, the Brillouin-amplified probe wave passes through a wave shaper (WS, Finisar Waveshaper 4000S) to remove the upper sideband, then routed to a $1 2 5 ~ \mathrm { M H z }$ ultrasensitive photodetector (PD, New Focus 1811-FC). The electrical signals of the PD are sampled by a real-time sampling oscilloscope with a sampling rate of $1 \mathrm { G S a } / s ,$ and processed offline using a MATLAB program. To measure the full-band Brillouin spectrum, we developed a LabVIEW-based program to sweep the probe sideband frequency $\nu _ { F }$ with a step size of 1 MHz. Meanwhile, the software synchronously acquires the Stokes signal’s data from the oscilloscope at each $\nu _ { F }$ . To reduce the PDG-induced fluctuation, 100 times averaging is employed. Finally, by Lorentzian fitting at different pulse times, the BFS distribution along the fiber with 1 m spatial resolution and 1 MHz frequency resolution is retrieved.

In the BOTDA system, both ends of the FMF are centralaligned and taper spliced with the SMF-based components to excite the fundamental mode in FMF. The schematic diagram of the taper spliced area of FMF and SMF is shown in Fig. 3(a), and Fig. 3(b) shows the microscope image of the taper spliced area. Thanks to the fusion taper processing, the whole splicing loss induced by core diameter mismatch of SMF and FMF is reduced from 3 dB to less than 0.4 dB, which improves the sensitivity and SNR of our system.

First, we calibrate the BFS variation coefficient in Eq. (4) in the FMF. The calibration diagram is shown in Fig. 4; half of the FMF (total length ${ \approx } 8 \mathrm { ~ m }$ ) is carefully rolled around a foam plastic cylinder to avoid artificial stress, while the other half of the FMF stays stress free as a reference to compensate the temperature-variation-induced BFS variation. Considering the optical transmission loss is obvious increased when we rolled the $4 \textrm { m }$ FMF around a $0 . 7 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ radius foam plastic cylinder, we choose four foam plastic cylinders with bend radii of 0.9, 1.5, 1.9, and $2 . 5 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ , whose transmission losses are 1.5, 1.3, 1.1, and 0.8 dB, respectively, for BFS calibration.

(5), where $H$ represent the cylinder’s height, $L$ is the measured fiber length, $R _ { d }$ is the cylinder’ radius, and $\smash { \mathop { D _ { f } } }$ is the fiber diameter, which equals $2 5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ including the fiber coating:

$$

$$

The calculated radii and heights of every cylinder of the model are shown in Fig. 10. The errors increase for the cylinders with larger radius, because the BFS variation is diminished with the fiber curvature radius increasing, as Fig. 7 shows. Moreover, for the larger radius cylinders, the precise BFS is difficult to acquire because our BOTDA system’s frequency resolution is limited to $1 \ : \mathrm { M H z }$ , so the maximum measurable radius for our system is $3 4 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ according to Eq. (4). The minimum measurable radius is about 7 mm due to the bending loss. The height error comes from the combination of the radius inaccuracy and rough spatial resolution (1 m) of our BOTDA system.

Using the calculated radii and heights, the three-dimensional shape of the model can be rebuilt as shown in Fig. 11. The blue semitransparent color represents the real model, and the gold color represents the rebuilt model. The rebuilt model generally overlaps with the original one, which proves the FMF-BOTDAbased distributed curvature measurement is effective.

In conclusion, we proposed and investigated an innovative distributed curvature fiber sensor using an FMF-based BOTDA system under the quasi-single mode operation. Through the system, a 1 MHz spectrum step and $1 \textrm { m }$ spatial resolution have been successfully implemented.
